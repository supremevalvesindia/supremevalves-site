Technical FAQs — Valves & Strainers

How do I select valve materials for sea‑water service?

Use corrosion‑resistant alloys such as SS316/SS904L or Cu‑Ni for wetted parts. Avoid plain carbon steel internals. Consider cathodic protection and crevice‑resistant designs. Refer to our Materials page for a media vs. material matrix.

What is the difference between ASME Class and PN ratings?

ASME Classes (e.g., 150/300/600) are pressure–temperature systems defined in ASME B16.34 tables. PN ratings (e.g., PN16/PN25/PN40) are nominal pressure ratings in bar under EN standards. They are not directly interchangeable; consult the PT tables.

Which standards apply to face‑to‑face dimensions?

Commonly ASME B16.10 for valves and manufacturer standards for strainers. Always verify per valve type and standard specified in the datasheet.

What testing is performed on valves?

Hydrostatic shell and seat tests per API 598 (or ISO 5208/EN 12266). Special tests such as FE (fugitive emissions), cryogenic, and fire‑safe can be provided on request.

How do I size an electric or pneumatic actuator?

Base sizing on maximum differential pressure, media factor, and valve torque/thrust with safety margin. Include fail‑safe position, travel time, and control signal requirements.

What mesh size should I use for a Y or basket strainer?

Typically select 2–3 times smaller than the minimum particle size to protect downstream equipment. Balance pressure drop against filtration fineness. Provide DP ports and set a cleaning threshold (e.g., 0.5 bar).

Can I install a valve in vertical pipelines?

Yes, but check the valve type. Check valves and strainers have preferred flow directions; orientation affects performance and maintenance access.

What documents are supplied with valves?

Standard: hydro test reports, MTCs (EN 10204 3.1), GA drawings. On request: IBR, NACE/MRO175 compliance, fire‑safe, PMI, FE tests, and ATEX/IECEx for actuators.

How do I mitigate water hammer?

Use non‑slam/nozzle check valves, slow‑closing actuators, surge tanks, and proper line sizing. Verify transient analysis for critical services.

What is the difference between floating and trunnion ball valves?

Floating: ball is supported by seats and moves to seal; suited for small/medium sizes, moderate pressures. Trunnion: ball is supported by trunnions; lower operating torque, better for large sizes and higher pressures.
